Part One

Clear & Compelling

Before we can teach anything, the message has to land. We reviewed the framework we carry, the 4 M's in my pocket. Tap each one.

What?
Message
State the expectation simply, with no jargon and no assumed context. Ground it in the mission.
How?
Method
Choose a format intentionally, phone, huddle, email, O3, CTM. Order matters, and each format signals something different.
Who and why?
Match
Know who you are communicating with, and why it will matter to them specifically. Relationships are key.
Wow?
Magic
Win hearts and minds so the recipient feels seen, valued, uniquely capable, and inspired. Spark emotional investment.

Then we put it to work. Each team drafted a clear and compelling message about the behaviors aligned to TEAM, with the undercurrent of Excellence for our improvement. Here is the one we landed on.

Team,

For almost twenty years, this school has done hard things for our kids, and we have never done them alone. We have done them together, as a team, and that is exactly why, as we step into Year 20, the value we are choosing to lead us forward is the one that has quietly carried every theme and every season we have lived here: TEAM.

Each of our values has gotten a serious glow up, and when I look closely at our TEAM value, the behavior that matters most right now is candor: making sure each and every one of us is consistently saying the thing that needs to be said, with care, on behalf of our kids and our staff. This is how we live out the phrases we hold dear, the ones we say to each other and to our students: that we are a TEAM and a family, that this is OUR house, and that we can do hard things.

I will not pretend this will feel easy, because change rarely does. But I believe in the resilience of this community with my whole heart, and I am certain this is the right move at the right time.

I also owe you honesty. We are not yet where we need to be, and after almost twenty years, how we treat one another should match the longevity we are so proud of. Let me be clear about what this is and what it is not. It is not a question of love, because this is the most kid-centered staff I could ever hope to lead. It is a question of the behaviors that allow a strong team to last. This is also how we live Excellence, because excellence is continuous improvement, and there is no improving without holding ourselves and one another honestly accountable.

We already produce results, in our GPA goals, our four-year matriculation rate, our AP and dual-enrollment offerings, our attendance, and our course goals. So imagine how much further Year 20 can carry us when we also celebrate what is great, know each other as people, have each other's back, name what is not working and bring the fix, and stand behind every decision once it is made, because all of it comes back to what is best for our kids.

When we closed last year, I left you with a single question: what would it mean if we were the best school? I told you then that we were chasing the ideal, and I am telling you now that the chase is not over. This, the way we treat each other, is how we close the distance.

This is our house. Let us build it into the school our children deserve, in how we treat each other, every single day.

So I will leave you with this: what does this look like for you, in your individual role, in your department, on your grade level, in your championship, and in our whole-school spaces?

Part Two

Teaching with Precision

How we launched

We compared two coaches from The Devil Wears Prada: Miranda and Nigel. Miranda holds a sky-high bar but leaves people to figure it out, often through fear. Nigel holds the same bar and actually teaches, he explains the why and walks alongside you. The question we sat with: which coach are we when we develop our people?

Two quotes were on the board:

We often believe that we were clearer than we actually were. We can over-index on what we know we meant, rather than how it was actually heard.
The single biggest problem in communication is the illusion that it has taken place.On communication

Then we practiced two moves:

Reps

Give feedback in bite-sized pieces. Master one before you add the next.

Getting meta

Walk the person you coach through your own thinking, out loud, so they learn how you think, not just what you concluded.

Then we formed 3 teams and built a plan to teach this with precision: first name the skill gap, then name how we would teach the skill.

Read our teaching plan (CLICK HERE)›

The gap

This is not a commitment gap. It is a skill gap in candor: giving direct, specific, behavior-based feedback to peers as much as to direct reports, going to the person closest to the problem instead of around them, receiving feedback without shutting down, and aligning behind a decision once it is made.

How we teach it

Teach one shared feedback frame and practice it live. Build feedback into every meeting, both ways, affirming and adjusting. Adopt the direct rule, a concern about a teammate is handled with that teammate, not around them. Celebrate the wins out loud. And strategize by group, naming the feedback culture early for new staff, breaking the save-it-for-people-below-us habit for returning staff, and calibrating monthly as leaders.

The full plan, with every skill gap and teaching move, lives in our Day 2 working doc.

We know each other as people.
We are more than our titles and our rooms. We make the time to know the person behind the work, not just the work.
We shine a light on the wins.
We catch each other doing it right and we say so, out loud and often. Naming what is working is as much our job as naming what is not.
We keep what happens in our team, in our team.
What is said in the room stays in the room. That is what makes it safe to be honest with each other.
We lead our rooms out loud.
When something in my room is not working, I say it first, early, and plainly. When it sits in yours, I come to you to help, not around you to talk about it.
We bring the solution with the problem.
If it is mine to carry, I show up with a first step, not just the weight of it. Venting names the problem. It does not move it.
We offer help before anyone asks.
I do not WAIT to be asked. I notice where you are stretched and I step in. I put my hand up in the hallway, and I say yes when yours is up.
We say the actual thing, and we say it with care.
Frank and kind are not opposites. If I have feedback, I give you the real version in plain words. No hinting, no talking around it, no "my way or nothing." I take any personnel issue, concern, or important update straight to the person who needs it, never around them. The only test is what is best for our kids and for this team.
We disagree in the room, then we rally.
I bring my honest take while the call is still open. Once it is made, I carry it like I made it. No "I knew that would not work" in the hallway after.
We treat whole-school work as ours.
Every staff-facing and school-facing event, initiative, and decision gets my full weight, whether or not my name is on it.

The undercurrent: Excellence

Excellence is what keeps us honest about results. It means catching a problem while it is small, fixing it, and offering a hand before anyone has to ask. It also means EVERYONE is held accountable to the urgent pursuit of results. And it begins with each of us: we always use the mirror before the magnifying glass. As Maya Angelou said, when you know better, do better.

What this makes possible for the full school
  • We become one, and staff feel the shift in adult culture before we ever name it.
  • You get the same answer from any one of us. That consistency is what makes people feel safe.
  • Because we are aligned, any of us can give any of us feedback, and it lands as care.
